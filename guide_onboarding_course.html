The Learning Guide Role

📖 ~14 min✦ Quiz required

Who Is a Learning Guide?

A learning guide is the heart of the Helon Universe micro-school. You are not a teacher in the traditional sense — you are a personalized instructional partner. You work closely with a small group of learners (maximum 4), delivering 1:1 curriculum instruction, supporting independent digital learning stations, tracking progress, and communicating with families.

Your learners look to you for structure, safety, consistency, and challenge. You are often the most significant adult learning relationship in their day. That is a profound responsibility — and an equally profound opportunity.

What You Do Each Day

Direct Instruction

Deliver curriculum lessons 1:1 during Teacher blocks. Each learner receives two Teacher sessions per day. This is where MBTP, Curriculum Access, and direct skill instruction happen.

Facilitate Independent Stations

Set learners up on Lexia Core5 and LittleLit stations. Monitor engagement, check progress data after each session, and intervene when needed.

Observe & Document

Notice what's happening beyond the screen data — engagement, frustration, breakthroughs, confusion. Record observations in your daily log.

Plan Ahead

Use the end-of-day log and planning tool to plan tomorrow's sessions based on today's data. Never arrive at a session without knowing exactly what you're teaching.

Communicate with Families

Generate a daily parent report from your log. Families depend on you to bridge the gap between what happens in the micro-school and what they understand at home.

Grow Professionally

Complete program training courses, stay current on learner progress, and reflect on your own practice. Great guides are lifelong learners themselves.

What Makes a Great Guide

The most effective learning guides share a few key qualities: they are consistent (learners trust the routine), curious (they genuinely want to understand each learner), data-driven (they make decisions based on evidence, not assumptions), and warm (they build the relationship that makes learning possible).

Remember: You don't have to know everything on day one. You DO need to show up prepared, follow the systems, ask questions, and stay curious about your learners. The tools will support you — your job is to show up and care deeply.

The HLPP System

📖 ~15 min✦ Quiz required

Helon Universe

HLPP (Helon Universe) is the instructional planning system that your micro-school runs on. It is built around a four-station rotation model — a structured daily schedule where each learner rotates between four types of learning time.

The Four Stations

🌟 Teacher 1:1

Direct instruction from the guide. This is where curriculum lessons (MBTP or Curriculum Access) happen. Each learner gets 2 Teacher blocks per day.

💻 Lexia Core5

Adaptive phonics and decoding program. The learner works independently on the digital platform. Guide monitors from a distance and checks reports after.

📖 LittleLit

Independent digital reading program. Leveled books with comprehension activities. The learner works alone while the guide is in a Teacher block with another learner.

📚 Independent Work

Reinforcement of the day's curriculum lessons — worksheets, activities, review tasks. Student completes independently when not in another station.

Scheduling Rules You Must Know

Non-Negotiable Rules

  • You can teach only one student at a time — one student per Teacher block.
  • Every learner must receive 2 Teacher blocks per day — no exceptions.
  • Every learner must have at least 1 Lexia Core5 session per day.
  • Every learner must have at least 1 LittleLit session per day.
  • Maximum students = 4. Beyond 4, there are not enough Teacher blocks.
  • Daily order: Core5 first → LittleLit independent → Teacher 1:1 (curriculum).

The Mastery Rubric

All curriculum progress in HLPP is evaluated using the same four-level rubric:

LevelDescription
ExploresInitial exposure — the learner is encountering this concept or skill for the first time
DevelopsSkill development — the learner is building toward mastery with support
AppliesConsistent performance — the learner demonstrates the skill reliably with little support
IntegratesMastery and transfer — the learner applies the skill independently in new contexts
The 80% rule: Any skill area where a learner performs below 80% — in any program — should be treated as an intervention priority. Flag it, plan a response, and document it in the parent report.

The Programs at a Glance

Four Programs, One Complete Learner

Every program in the HLPP system addresses a specific dimension of literacy and learning. Understanding what each program does — and doesn't do — helps you use them together strategically.

MBTP vs. Curriculum Access — Which One?

A learner uses either MBTP or Curriculum Access as their primary curriculum — not both simultaneously as their main program. The guide determines which based on the learner's needs:

Use MBTP when:

The learner engages well with discussion, can access leveled literature independently (with support), and is ready for concept-based, humanities-rich instruction.

Use Curriculum Access when:

The learner needs more structure and visual support than MBTP provides, has an uneven skill profile, benefits from functional skills instruction, or is an autistic learner.

Data, Progress & Planning

📖 ~12 min✦ Quiz required

The Daily Data Loop

The HLPP system runs on a daily data loop: you deliver instruction, observe and document what happens, analyze the data, plan tomorrow's sessions, and communicate with families. This loop happens every single day — not once a week, not at the end of the month.

1

Deliver Sessions

Teach curriculum, facilitate digital stations, observe engagement and behavior.

2

Check Program Data

After every Core5 session, pull the progress report. After every LittleLit session, check comprehension scores. After curriculum, note mastery level.

3

Log the Day

Complete the end-of-day log in the Daily Planning tool. Document all three programs plus your observations. Upload any reports or work samples.

4

Generate Tomorrow's Plan

Use the AI planning tool to generate a prioritized plan for tomorrow based on today's data. Review it, adjust if needed, and note prep required.

5

Send the Parent Report

Generate and send (or share) the parent report. Families should receive this daily — it is your primary communication tool.

What Belongs in Your Daily Log

SectionWhat to Document
MBTP / Curriculum AccessUnit, lesson, option used, mastery level, what was covered, wins, struggles
Lexia Core5Minutes, score, level, skills worked on, wins, any skill alerts below 80%
LittleLitMinutes, comprehension score, book, level, wins, concerns
ObservationsEngagement, behavior patterns, emotional state, highlight moment, anything the data can't capture
The golden rule of documentation: If it isn't written down, it didn't happen. Your log is the institutional memory of your learner's progress. It protects the learner, supports families, and makes you a better guide.

Your First Week

📖 ~10 min✦ Final quiz

What to Do Before Day One

1

Complete Your Training Courses

Finish all program training courses — MBTP or Curriculum Access (whichever your learners use), Lexia Core5, LittleLit, and Supporting Autistic Learners (if relevant). Don't walk into a session with a program you haven't trained on.

2

Get Your Learner Profiles

Read every assessment, profile, or intake document available for each learner. Talk to the family. Find out strengths, interests, sensitivities, and what "a good day" looks like for this child.

3

Set Up Placement

Log into each program and confirm level placements. For Core5 and LittleLit, check the platform. For MBTP or CA, review the placement assessment results and confirm the starting lesson or level.

4

Prepare Your Physical Space

Materials in predictable places. Visual schedule posted. Data sheets ready. Devices charged. Nothing should be scrambled when the learner arrives.

5

Know Your First Day Plan

What is Block 1? Block 2? Who is in each station? What lesson are you teaching? What is the first thing a learner does when they walk in? Know this before day one.

First Week Mindset

Prioritize Routine Over Content

The first week is about establishing the routine — the schedule, the transitions, the expectations. Content will follow. A learner who knows what to expect every day is a learner who can learn.

Learn Each Learner

Your best instruction in week one is observation. What motivates them? What overwhelms them? What do they do when they're curious vs. stuck? This information is more valuable than any lesson plan.

Expect Adjustment

New routines take time. Expect some resistance, confusion, or unsteadiness — especially from learners who have experienced inconsistency before. Stay calm, stay consistent, stay warm.

Use the Daily Log from Day One

Start your documentation habit on the very first day, even if you only have a few notes. The habit is what matters — the completeness will build over time.

You are ready. The tools, the training, and the systems are in place. What matters most now is your consistency, your curiosity about your learners, and your commitment to showing up prepared every day. Welcome to the team.
