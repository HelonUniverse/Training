What Is LittleLit?

📖 ~12 min✦ Quiz required

A Program Built Around Independent Reading

LittleLit is a research-backed digital reading platform that builds independent reading skills, comprehension, and a love of books for learners in PreK through Grade 5. Unlike Core5, which focuses on decoding and phonics mechanics, LittleLit focuses on what happens after decoding — helping learners think deeply about what they read, respond to literature, and develop their reading identity.

Sessions in LittleLit pair a carefully leveled book with structured activities — response prompts, vocabulary exploration, comprehension questions, and creative extensions. The program is designed to be used independently by the learner while a guide is available for support and discussion.

What LittleLit Builds

Reading Stamina

Time reading independently in a focused, low-distraction environment with a real book — not a test.

Comprehension

Inferencing, main idea, character analysis, cause and effect — the skills that separate word-callers from true readers.

Vocabulary in Context

Words encountered naturally in books, with supported definition work that builds word knowledge alongside real reading.

Reader Identity

When learners read books they choose and respond to meaningfully, they begin to see themselves as readers. This is LittleLit's deepest goal.

Written Response

Short, structured written responses to reading — building the habit of articulating thinking about text.

Discussion Skills

Preparing learners for the literary conversations that MBTP's discussion-based sessions depend on.

Leveled Reading — How It Works

LittleLit assigns books based on the learner's reading level, using a leveling system (typically letter-based A–Z or equivalent). Learners are placed at a level where they can read with 90–95% accuracy — challenging enough to grow, accessible enough to succeed. As learners demonstrate comprehension and complete activities successfully, they advance through levels.

Key distinction: LittleLit is NOT a phonics program and is NOT a substitute for Core5. A learner who cannot yet decode fluently will struggle with LittleLit. Make sure Core5 sessions happen first — LittleLit assumes basic decoding is in place.

How LittleLit Works

📖 ~12 min✦ Quiz required

The Structure of a LittleLit Session

Each LittleLit session follows a consistent structure that learners internalize over time. This predictability is intentional — it builds routine, reduces cognitive load, and lets learners focus on the reading itself rather than figuring out what to do next.

1

Book Selection or Assignment

The learner opens their assigned book (or selects from leveled options). The book is appropriately leveled to their current reading level.

2

Independent Reading

The learner reads the assigned pages or chapters independently. For younger/beginning readers, guides may read alongside. Audio support may be available for accessibility.

3

Comprehension Check

Questions about the text — literal, inferential, evaluative. These help the program assess understanding and identify where comprehension is breaking down.

4

Vocabulary Activity

Key words from the text are explored in context. The learner encounters definitions, uses words in sentences, and builds word knowledge naturally.

5

Written or Creative Response

A short response prompt asks the learner to think beyond the literal text — predict, connect, evaluate, or imagine. This is the highest-order thinking in the session.

Session Length & Frequency

Recommended: 20–30 minutes per LittleLit session, 3–4 days per week. This sits within the HLPP schedule as the independent station — learners work on LittleLit while the guide works 1:1 with another learner.

Book Levels Explained

LittleLit uses a progressive leveling system. Key things to know:

On-Level Books

Books the learner can read with 90–95% accuracy. Most daily reading should be at this level.

Challenge Books

Slightly above level — with guide support. Not for independent reading. Good for read-aloud discussion.

Comfort Books

Below level — easy, fluent reading. Good for building stamina and confidence. Use occasionally, not daily.

Level Up Triggers

When a learner achieves consistent 85%+ comprehension scores across multiple books, they advance to the next level.

Guiding Discussion

📖 ~12 min✦ Quiz required

Why Discussion Matters

LittleLit builds solo reading skills. But the richest comprehension growth happens in conversation. When guides ask genuine, open-ended questions after a reading session — and listen to the learner's thinking — they build the analytical skills that will carry the learner through MBTP and beyond.

The key is moving beyond "Did you understand it?" toward questions that reveal and deepen thinking. This is called the Socratic approach — asking questions not to test, but to extend and challenge thinking.

Three Levels of Questions

1

Literal Questions (What happened?)

These confirm basic comprehension. Use sparingly — if a learner can't answer these, they need re-reading support, not higher-level discussion. Example: "What did the character do when she found the door?"

2

Inferential Questions (Why? How?)

These require reading between the lines. The answer isn't stated directly — the learner must synthesize information. Example: "Why do you think the character hesitated before going inside?" This is the heart of literary discussion.

3

Evaluative Questions (What do you think?)

These require judgment — the learner defends a view. Example: "Was the character right to keep the secret? Why or why not?" No single correct answer — the goal is reasoned thinking.

The guide's role in discussion: Ask. Listen. Follow up. Resist the urge to confirm or deny too quickly. When a learner gives an answer — whether right or wrong — ask "What makes you think that?" first. This habit develops intellectual independence.

Discussion Moves to Use

MoveWhen to Use ItExample
ExtendLearner gives a thin answer"Say more about that."
EvidenceLearner makes a claim"What in the book makes you think that?"
ChallengeLearner gives an easy/obvious answer"Could there be another explanation?"
ConnectLearner finishes a text"Does this remind you of anything in your own life?"
PredictMid-book pause"What do you think will happen next — and why?"

Reading the Progress Data

📖 ~10 min✦ Quiz required

What LittleLit Tracks

LittleLit generates data on reading activity, comprehension scores, vocabulary performance, and time-on-task. As a guide, you use this data to understand where a learner is thriving, where they're stuck, and what to address in direct instruction or discussion.

Books Completed

Total books read at each level. Low completion rate = learner may not be engaging with the station or struggling with the level.

Comprehension Scores

% correct on comprehension questions per book. Below 70% = learner may be reading without understanding — level may be too high.

Vocabulary Performance

Tracks which words have been mastered vs. still developing. Informs direct vocabulary instruction outside of LittleLit.

Time on Task

Minutes spent reading per session. Short sessions = learner may be clicking through without reading.

Key Thresholds to Watch

MetricConcern ThresholdGuide Response
Comprehension scoreBelow 70%Level may be too high. Consider dropping a level. Add pre-reading vocabulary support.
Books completedFewer than 2/weekCheck if learner is actually reading or skipping pages. Sit with them during part of the station.
Time on taskUnder 10 min/sessionLearner likely rushing. Establish a minimum reading time expectation.
Same level for 3+ weeksNo advancementReview comprehension scores. Offer guided discussion to boost understanding.
Remember the 80% rule: Any comprehension area consistently below 80% should be addressed through direct discussion and re-reading in addition to regular LittleLit sessions. Document in your daily planning log and mention in the parent report.

LittleLit + MBTP + Core5

📖 ~10 min✦ Final quiz

The Complete Literacy Picture

When LittleLit, Core5, and MBTP are used together intentionally, they address every dimension of literacy development. Understanding how they fit together helps you plan sessions that reinforce each other rather than feel disconnected.

ProgramWhat It BuildsStation Type
Core5Phonics, decoding, phonological awarenessDigital — semi-independent
LittleLitComprehension, vocabulary, reader identityDigital — fully independent
MBTPLiterary analysis, writing, integrated subjectsGuide-led — 1:1 instruction

How to Weave All Three Together

1

Core5 first in the day

Phonics warm-up prepares the brain for reading. This is always the first literacy activity.

2

LittleLit during independent station

While the guide works 1:1 on MBTP with another learner, this learner reads independently on LittleLit.

3

MBTP Language Arts with the guide

Guide-led instruction on the unit book. Discussion, activities, writing. Now the learner has both decoded (Core5) and read independently (LittleLit) as preparation.

4

Bridge vocabulary across all three

When a word appears in all three — in Core5 as a pattern, in LittleLit as a story word, in MBTP as a discussion term — point it out explicitly. These cross-program connections accelerate vocabulary acquisition.

Your End-of-Day Checklist (All Three Programs)

  • Core5: How many minutes? Any skill alerts? Skill area score below 80%?
  • LittleLit: Book completed? Comprehension score? Response quality? Time on task?
  • MBTP: Which lesson? Option 1 or 2? Discussion quality? Mastery level?
  • Cross-program: Any vocabulary, concept, or skill that bridges all three to document?
  • Tomorrow: What needs reinforcement? What is ready to advance?
