What Is Curriculum Access?

📖 ~12 min✦ Quiz required

A Curriculum Built for Learners with Diverse Needs

Curriculum Access (CA) is The Autism Helper's comprehensive, ready-to-use curriculum platform designed specifically for learners with autism and other special needs. It is used in thousands of classrooms worldwide and is equally well-suited to homeschool and micro-school settings like HLPP.

The key insight behind CA is that many learners with autism have scattered skills — they may be advanced in one area and significantly behind in another. Traditional grade-level curricula don't account for this. CA solves that problem by placing students at their skill level, not their grade level, and providing a clear, structured path forward across multiple subject areas simultaneously.

What Makes Curriculum Access Different

Structured & Routine-Based

Every lesson follows the same format. Predictable routines reduce anxiety, build independence, and free cognitive load for learning.

Skill-Level Placement

Students are placed based on what they know, not their age or grade. A 10-year-old might work at Level 2 in Language Arts and Level 4 in Math.

Data Embedded

Every unit includes pre/post assessments and rubrics. Data collection is built into the lesson — not an add-on.

Differentiation Built In

Up to 6 levels per subject, plus behavior and communication supports, visual supports, and extension activities.

Easy to Use

Implementation guides, built-in training videos, and ready-to-use materials mean less prep time and more instructional time.

Evidence-Based

Rooted in ABA principles, structured teaching, and other evidence-based practices for autism education.

Who Uses Curriculum Access?

CA was originally designed for special education classrooms but has expanded to serve a wide range of settings. In the HLPP model, CA is used for learners who need more structure, visual support, and skill-level differentiation than a typical literature-based curriculum like MBTP provides.

In your micro-school: A learner might use Curriculum Access instead of MBTP as their primary curriculum, or alongside MBTP for specific subject areas where they need more structured support. Your role as a guide is the same — structured 1:1 instruction during Teacher blocks.

Levels, Subjects & Placement

The 6 Levels

Curriculum Access offers up to 6 levels per subject. Levels do not correspond to grade levels — they represent a skill progression from foundational to more complex within that subject area. A learner might be at Level 1 in Language Arts and Level 3 in Math at the same time.

How Placement Works

1

Use the Skills Matrix

The Autism Helper provides a Skills Matrix for each subject. This is a pre-assessment tool that identifies which skills the learner has and which are emerging. Complete this before starting any subject.

2

Identify the Starting Level

Based on Skills Matrix results, identify the level where the learner has partial mastery — not where they fail everything, and not where they know everything already. The goal is productive challenge.

3

Place Each Subject Independently

A learner may be at different levels in different subjects. This is expected and normal. Document each subject's placement level in your planning notes.

4

Reassess Regularly

Each unit includes a post-test. When a learner passes a post-test with strong performance, they advance to the next level in that subject.

Key principle: Meet the learner where they are, not where you think they should be. It is always better to start slightly lower and build confidence than to start too high and create frustration.

Running a Lesson

📖 ~13 min✦ Quiz required

The Instructional Routine

Every Curriculum Access lesson follows the same sequence. This consistency is not accidental — it is one of the program's core strengths. When the routine is predictable, learners can focus their energy on the content rather than figuring out what comes next. Guides should follow this routine every session.

1

Preview / Anchor Chart

Open with the unit's anchor chart or key vocabulary. Briefly review what was covered in the previous session. Keep this to 2–3 minutes. The goal is to activate prior knowledge — not re-teach everything.

2

Model the Skill

Show the learner exactly how to complete today's task before asking them to try. Use the implementation guide language: "Watch me first." Do one complete example. This is Explicit Instruction — a core ABA-aligned practice.

3

Guided Practice

Do 1–2 examples together with the learner, providing prompts and support as needed. Fade prompts as the learner shows understanding. Note the level of prompting needed — this is data.

4

Independent Practice

The learner completes the lesson activity independently while you observe and collect data. Avoid over-helping — let them show you what they know. Use the lesson's data sheet.

5

Review & Correct

Go over responses together. Correct errors immediately with the correct answer and a brief re-model. Error correction is instructional — not a punishment or a setback.

6

Record & Wrap Up

Record data on the lesson's rubric or data sheet. Note accuracy rate, prompting level, and any errors. End with a positive close: "You worked hard today."

What's in Each Lesson Package

Implementation Guide

Step-by-step instructions for running the lesson. Follow this during the session — especially when you're new to a unit.

Student Materials

Worksheets, adapted books, file folders, or task cards for the learner. Print or prepare these before the session.

Anchor Chart / Visuals

Key vocabulary and concept visuals to post or display during instruction. These become familiar reference points across the unit.

Data Sheet

Pre-made data collection form. Record accuracy, errors, and prompting level. Required — not optional.

Prep before you teach: Read the implementation guide, print student materials, and have the data sheet ready BEFORE the session begins. Scrambling during instruction undermines the structured routine learners depend on.

Data Collection & Progress

📖 ~11 min✦ Quiz required

Why Data Matters More Here

In a standard classroom, a teacher might intuitively know how 25 students are doing. In a 1:1 or micro-school setting, data is how you demonstrate progress, make instructional decisions, and communicate with families. CA makes this easy by including pre-made data sheets with every lesson.

The Three Data Points to Collect Every Session

Data PointWhat to RecordWhy It Matters
Accuracy Rate% of items correct during independent practice (e.g., 7/10 = 70%)Tells you if the learner is ready to advance or needs more practice
Prompt LevelHow much support was needed: Independent / Verbal / Gestural / PhysicalTracks growing independence — goal is always to fade prompts
Error PatternsWhich specific items were missed — note the type of errorInforms what to re-teach and whether a different approach is needed

Pre-Tests, Post-Tests & Level Advancement

1

Pre-Test Before Each Unit

Give the unit pre-test before starting any lessons. This confirms the placement level is correct and identifies specific skills the learner already knows (these can be skipped or taught quickly).

2

Collect Daily Data

Use the provided data sheet every session. Record accuracy and prompting. Never rely on memory — even experienced guides lose track of details without a data system.

3

Post-Test at Unit End

Administer the unit post-test when all lessons are complete. Compare to the pre-test. Strong post-test performance (80%+) signals readiness to advance to the next level in this subject.

4

Make Data-Based Decisions

If a learner scores below 70% on multiple lessons in a row, the level may be too high — consider dropping one level or re-teaching the unit's foundational lessons. If they're consistently above 90% with no prompting, consider advancing.

The 80% rule applies here too: Any skill area below 80% accuracy for two or more consecutive sessions should trigger re-teaching, not just more practice of the same material.

Using Data in Parent Reports

Your CA data directly feeds the parent report you generate in the Daily Planning tool. When you document accuracy scores and prompt levels, you are creating the evidence base for everything you communicate to families. This data is what separates a professional learning guide from a babysitter — use it every session.

Curriculum Access in the HLPP Model

📖 ~10 min✦ Final quiz

Where CA Fits in the Rotation

Just like MBTP, Curriculum Access happens during the Teacher 1:1 block in the HLPP rotation. CA is never an independent station — it requires your active instruction, prompting, and data collection. Each learner using CA should receive their two Teacher blocks per day dedicated to CA lessons (or a mix of CA subjects).

MBTPCurriculum Access
DeliveryGuide-led 1:1 instructionGuide-led 1:1 instruction
Best forLearners ready for literature-based, discussion-driven learningLearners who need more structure, visual support, skill-level differentiation
SubjectsLanguage Arts, Science, Social Studies (thematic)9 subjects including Functional Math, Life Skills, Functional Literacy
Data systemMastery rubric (Explores→Integrates)Pre/post tests, accuracy rates, prompt levels
Schedule slotTeacher 1:1 blocksTeacher 1:1 blocks

When to Use CA Instead of MBTP

1

Learner needs more structure than MBTP provides

MBTP assumes a certain level of language and discussion ability. If a learner struggles to engage with discussion-based instruction, CA's explicit instruction model is a better fit.

2

Learner needs functional skills alongside academics

CA's Life Skills, Functional Math, and Functional Literacy subjects address real-world independence. MBTP does not cover these areas.

3

Learner benefits from visual supports built into the curriculum

CA includes anchor charts, visual supports, and adapted materials by default. MBTP requires the guide to adapt materials separately.

Using CA and Core5 + LittleLit Together

  • CA's Phonics subject and Lexia Core5 are complementary — both target decoding. CA Phonics is guide-led; Core5 is the independent digital reinforcement. Use both for learners who need strong phonics scaffolding.
  • CA's Reading Comprehension and LittleLit overlap in purpose but approach it differently. CA RC uses adapted texts with more scaffold; LittleLit uses leveled independent reading. Both can run simultaneously.
  • The daily schedule remains the same: Core5 first → LittleLit independent station → CA 1:1 with guide (in Teacher block).
  • Document CA data in the Daily Planning tool just as you would MBTP — in the "MBTP / Curriculum" section.
